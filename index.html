What is ISO 9001?

ISO 9001 is the world's most recognised Quality Management System (QMS) standard. It gives your organisation a clear framework to consistently deliver high-quality services — and to keep improving. In a hospital like SKMCH&RC, it means every patient gets the same excellent care, every time.

Chapter 1

Why does ISO 9001 matter?

Before diving into the standard, let's understand the problem it solves — and why hospitals across the world adopt it.

1
Consistency — Same quality, every single time
▶

Without a system, quality depends on who is working that day. ISO 9001 builds processes so the outcome is predictable, reliable, and repeatable — regardless of which nurse, doctor, or admin is on shift.

Hospital Example

A patient receiving chemotherapy gets the same safety checks, dosage verification, and monitoring protocol — whether it is Monday morning or Friday night.

🏥 SKMCH&RC relevance: Treatment pathways, documentation standards
2
Risk Reduction — Stop problems before they happen
▶

ISO 9001 requires organisations to think ahead — identify risks and put controls in place before failures occur. This is called risk-based thinking.

Hospital Example

Before introducing a new lab test, the team analyses what could go wrong — sample mix-ups, equipment failure, staff training gaps — and builds safeguards in advance.

3
Continuous Improvement — Always getting better
▶

ISO 9001 is not a one-time certificate. It drives a culture where every department measures performance, learns from mistakes, and improves — month after month, year after year.

Hospital Example

The QI team at SKMCH&RC tracks KPIs on run charts, spots trends, and runs improvement projects when targets are not met. That is ISO 9001 in action.

4
Patient Trust — Proof you care
▶

ISO 9001 certification is internationally recognised proof that an organisation meets strict quality standards. For patients, it means the hospital has been independently verified to deliver safe, consistent care.

Hospital Example

When a patient travels from another city or country to SKMCH&RC, the ISO certification assures them that the care will meet global standards — not just local norms.

Chapter 2

The 7 Quality Principles

ISO 9001 is built on seven core principles. Think of these as the values your organisation must live by — not just policies on paper.

1
Customer Focus — The patient is always first
▶

Everything the organisation does must ultimately serve the patient (customer). Their needs drive decisions, process designs, and improvement priorities.

In Practice

Before redesigning the appointment booking process, survey patients about their pain points. Their feedback shapes the solution.

2
Leadership — Management sets the tone
▶

Senior leaders must visibly commit to quality — not just sign documents. They set direction, allocate resources, and make quality a strategic priority.

In Practice

The hospital CEO personally reviews quality dashboards monthly and chairs the Quality Committee. Their presence signals that quality is non-negotiable.

3
Engagement of People — Every person matters
▶

Quality cannot be owned by one department. Every staff member — from surgeons to housekeeping — plays a role and must understand how their work affects quality.

In Practice

Ward nurses document adverse events. Porters report broken equipment. The QI team trains all staff, not just clinicians, on quality reporting tools.

4
Process Approach — Think in systems, not silos
▶

Instead of focusing on individual tasks, ISO 9001 requires you to map and manage end-to-end processes. Outputs of one step become inputs to the next.

In Practice

The "patient journey" from registration → triage → consultation → investigation → treatment → discharge is mapped as one connected process, identifying handover risks at each step.

5
Improvement — Never stand still
▶

Continuous improvement is hardwired into ISO 9001. You must set objectives, measure results, find gaps, and fix them — in a never-ending cycle.

6
Evidence-Based Decisions — Data over gut feeling
▶

Decisions must be based on data analysis, not assumptions. This is where your OMAC Analytics dashboards directly support ISO 9001 compliance.

In Practice

When the turn-around time KPI shows a deteriorating trend on the run chart, the team investigates the root cause using data — not guesswork.

7
Relationship Management — Partners in quality
▶

Quality extends beyond your walls. Suppliers, partners, and contractors must also meet your standards — because their quality directly affects your patients.

In Practice

A reagent supplier for the lab is evaluated not just on price but on delivery reliability, quality certifications, and batch consistency.

Chapter 3

The PDCA Improvement Cycle

The engine of ISO 9001 is a four-step method called PDCA — Plan, Do, Check, Act. Click each phase to learn how it works in your hospital.

P
PLAN — Define the problem and design the solution
▶

Set your objective. Gather data to understand the current situation. Identify root causes and design a change that should improve things. Write it down.

Hospital Example

Target: Reduce average oncology treatment initiation time from 42 days to 30 days. Root cause analysis identifies appointment scheduling delays as the primary bottleneck. Plan: pilot a dedicated booking slot for new oncology referrals.

D
DO — Implement on a small scale first
▶

Run your change as a pilot — on one ward, one team, or one month. Collect data. Document what actually happens versus what you planned.

Hospital Example

The new booking system is piloted in the Breast Cancer Clinic for one month. Staff are trained, data is collected weekly, and issues are logged in real time.

C
CHECK — Did it work? Compare data to the plan
▶

Analyse the results of the pilot. Did the change produce the expected improvement? Use run charts, statistical tests, and audits to make an honest assessment.

Hospital Example

The run chart shows average initiation time dropped from 42 to 31 days. No increase in booking errors was detected. The change appears effective.

A
ACT — Standardise if it worked, revise if it didn't
▶

If the change worked — roll it out fully, update SOPs, train all staff, and hold the gain. If it did not work — learn from the failure and return to Plan with new knowledge.

Hospital Example

The new booking protocol is adopted hospital-wide. The SOP is updated, staff are trained, and the target is now tightened to 28 days for the next PDCA cycle.

Chapter 4

ISO 9001 — 7 Key Clauses Explained Simply

The standard is divided into 10 clauses. Clauses 4–10 are the "doing" clauses. Here they are in plain English.

4
Context of the Organisation — Know your world
▶

Understand who you are, who your stakeholders are (patients, staff, regulators, donors), and what internal/external factors affect your ability to deliver quality.

Simple Meaning

"What are the challenges and opportunities in our environment that could affect our quality?" — e.g., high patient volume, donor expectations, regulatory requirements.

5
Leadership — Top management must own quality
▶

Senior management must demonstrate leadership, establish a quality policy, assign clear roles, and ensure the QMS has resources to function.

Simple Meaning

The CEO and HODs cannot delegate quality entirely downward. They must visibly lead it, review it, and resource it.

6
Planning — Risks and objectives before action
▶

Before doing anything, identify risks and opportunities. Set measurable quality objectives. Plan how to achieve them.

Simple Meaning

Every year, set 3–5 quality objectives with targets, owners, and timelines. Then plan the actions needed to hit them.

7
Support — People, tools, and communication
▶

Ensure staff have the competence, awareness, and resources to do their jobs well. Control documents and records properly.

Simple Meaning

Staff must be trained. SOPs must be current. Information must flow. Records must be retrievable. Without support, even the best system fails.

8
Operation — Delivering care as planned
▶

Control your operational processes — from receiving a patient to delivering care. Control suppliers. Manage non-conformances (things that go wrong).

Simple Meaning

This is the "doing" clause. Follow your processes. When something goes wrong, document it and address it — do not ignore it.

9
Performance Evaluation — Measure everything meaningful
▶

Monitor and measure your processes. Conduct internal audits. Hold management reviews. Analyse data to understand if you are meeting your quality objectives.

Simple Meaning

Your OMAC KPI dashboards and run charts directly fulfil this clause — you are already doing ISO 9001 Performance Evaluation.

10
Improvement — Fix, learn, and advance
▶

When you find non-conformances, take corrective action. When you find opportunities, act on them. Continually improve the QMS itself.

Simple Meaning

Non-conformance ≠ failure. It is data. The question is: what did you do about it? Documented corrective actions are the gold standard.

Chapter 5

Audits & Management Reviews

Two of the most important verification tools in ISO 9001 — and how they work in a hospital setting.

1
Internal Audit — Checking yourself honestly
▶

Your own trained auditors check whether processes are being followed as documented. It is not about blame — it is about finding gaps before an external auditor does.

Key Point

Internal auditors should be independent — they should not audit their own department. Findings must be documented and followed up with corrective actions.

2
External / Certification Audit — Proving it to the world
▶

An accredited certification body (like Bureau Veritas or SGS) visits your organisation, reviews evidence, interviews staff, and determines whether you deserve the ISO 9001 certificate.

Key Point

Auditors look for evidence — documented procedures, completed forms, records of corrective actions. "We always do it that way" is not evidence. Documentation is.

3
Management Review — Leadership's formal check-in
▶

At least once a year, senior leadership must formally review the QMS — looking at audit results, KPI trends, customer feedback, risks, and resource needs.

Hospital Example

The SKMCH&RC Quality Committee meets quarterly. The agenda follows ISO 9001's management review inputs: KPI performance, audit findings, patient complaints, improvement opportunities.

4
Non-Conformance & Corrective Action
▶

When something does not meet requirements, it is a "non-conformance". ISO 9001 requires you to: contain it, investigate the root cause, take corrective action, and verify the action worked.

Simple Formula

NC found → contain immediately → root cause analysis (why did it happen?) → corrective action → verification → close. Every step documented.

ISO 9001 at SKMCH&RC

How does everything we have learned connect directly to the work you do every day in the Quality & Patient Safety Department?

★
Your KPI Dashboards = Clause 9 Performance Evaluation
▶

Every indicator you track — infection rates, turn-around times, treatment initiation days — is ISO 9001 performance monitoring. Your run charts provide the visual evidence auditors want to see.

OMAC Connection

The OMAC Integrated Scorecard directly fulfils ISO 9001 Clause 9.1 — monitoring and measurement of processes. Present it in your management review and you have audit-ready evidence.

★
QI Projects = PDCA Cycles in Action
▶

Every QI project — from improving hand hygiene compliance to reducing medication errors — follows the PDCA cycle. ISO 9001 does not prescribe which problems to fix; it requires you to have a systematic way of fixing them.

OMAC Connection

The Closure Screener module tracks whether each indicator's improvement actions have been completed. That is ISO 9001 Clause 10.2 — corrective action management.

★
Patient Complaints = External Feedback (Clause 9.1.2)
▶

ISO 9001 requires you to monitor customer (patient) perception. Patient satisfaction surveys, complaint logs, and feedback forms are all ISO 9001-compliant tools when systematically analysed.

★
SOPs & Policies = Documented Information (Clause 7.5)
▶

Every SOP, protocol, checklist, and policy is "documented information" under ISO 9001. They must be version-controlled, approved, accessible to staff, and reviewed periodically.

Practical Tip

When an SOP is updated, old versions must be withdrawn so staff are not following outdated guidance. Document who approved the new version and when.
